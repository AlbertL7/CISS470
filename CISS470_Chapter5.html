Five Critical Pitfalls to Avoid Part 2 of 6

⚠️ Common Measurement Mistakes

Pitfall 1: Irrelevant Metrics ▶

The "Millions of Attacks" Problem

Many reports reference millions of attacks per week/day. While eye-catching, this is entirely irrelevant.

  • Overwhelming majority from "script kiddies"
  • Minimally competent security deflects these easily
  • Tiny percentage from sophisticated attackers represents true risk

Better Metric: Focus on sophisticated attack attempts and their success rates

Pitfall 2: Only Lagging Indicators ▶

Looking Backward vs. Forward

Frequency and severity of incidents = important but inherently lagging

  • Represents outputs, not inputs
  • Can't be directly affected by management
  • Doesn't help prevent future incidents

Better Approach: Balance with leading indicators like patch currency, training completion

Pitfall 3: Assuming More is Better ▶

The Over-Control Trap

Organizations can incur too much cost and create too much complexity

  • Not every piece of data needs encryption
  • Not every system needs two-factor authentication
  • 10 years ago, more controls made sense
  • Today, balance is critical

Better Strategy: Risk-based approach with differentiated controls

Pitfall 4: Relying on Subjectivity ▶

The Red/Yellow/Green Problem

Subjective color coding lacks credibility with senior management

  • Even with best intentions, subjective assessments unreliable
  • Doesn't drive decisions effectively
  • No clear action items

Better Method: Tie colors to specific measurable targets or milestones

Pitfall 5: Measuring Only the Security Team ▶

The 80% Problem

80% of security happens outside the CISO's organization

  • Security team can't write secure code for developers
  • Can't apply patches for data center managers
  • Easy to focus on what security team does directly
  • Miss measuring enterprise resilience

Better Focus: Measure organizational security posture, not just security team activities

Four Components of Effective Cybersecurity Strategy Part 3 of 6

🎯 Building Your Strategy Foundation

Core Principle

Organizations can measure cybersecurity performance only in the context of a cybersecurity strategy that tightly connects with overall business strategy.

📋 The Four Essential Components

Component 1: Organization Risk Assessment ▶

Critical Questions to Answer:

  • Which data is most critical to the business?
  • What's most attractive to attackers?
  • Is customer data more sensitive than intellectual property?
  • What types of IP are most important?
  • How does this vary by region or line of business?

Remember: "Little minds try to defend everything at once" - Frederick the Great

Component 2: Cybersecurity Capabilities ▶

Seven Hallmarks of Digital Resilience

1. Prioritize information assets based on business risks
2. Differentiate protection based on asset importance
3. Integrate cybersecurity into enterprise-wide risk management
4. Enlist front-line personnel to protect assets they use
5. Integrate cybersecurity into technology environment
6. Deploy active defenses to uncover attacks proactively
7. Test continuously to improve incident response

Constraints to Consider:

  • Organizational change appetite
  • Skill sophistication of security team
  • Importance of sensitive information
  • Attacker sophistication level
  • Regulatory scrutiny
  • Customer expectations
Component 3: Target State Protections ▶

Creating Protection Tiers

Organizations must create tiers of protection spanning multiple controls:

  • What information assets will be encrypted?
  • How tightly should access be controlled?
  • Which systems need segregated networks?
  • Where to push for secure coding practices?

Example Tier System:

Tier Data Type Protection Requirements
Tier 3 High-margin pricing strategies Encryption at rest + 2FA
Tier 2 Customer data Encryption + access logs
Tier 1 Public information Basic access controls
Component 4: Portfolio of Initiatives ▶

Each Initiative Should Include:

  • Future state aspiration: Clear vision of success
  • Required funding: Budget allocation
  • Management support: Executive sponsorship
  • Required skills: Capability gaps to fill
  • Key milestones: Measurable progress points
  • Timing: Realistic implementation schedule

Types of Initiatives:

Enabling Initiatives
  • Managed services expansion
  • Team talent enhancement
  • Business skills training
  • Communication improvement
Governance Initiatives
  • Business leader involvement
  • Decision-making structures
  • Strategy alignment
  • Ongoing review mechanisms

Creating Your Performance Management System Part 4 of 6

📊 Three-Component Measurement Framework

The Complete System

To effectively manage cybersecurity strategy success, organizations need three complementary measurement components working together.

📈 Component 1: Measuring Progress Against Initiatives

Setting Medium-Term Success Metrics ▶

Example Initiative Metrics (2-3 year window):

DLP System: Decrease incidents to <100 per annum

Patch Management: Achieve 90% currency on external-facing systems within 2 years

Secure Development: 100% of projects include security in first 10% of lifecycle

Attack Prevention: Stop >99% of detectable attacks by year-end

Attribution: Identify attacker within one quarter for high-priority attacks

Training: 80% manager attendance at advanced cyber awareness annually

Phishing: Click-through rates <30% on annual tests

Using Markers as Milestones ▶

Example DLP Implementation Markers:

  1. Marker 1: DLP system installed with dedicated team
  2. Marker 2: System tuned with rules to prevent inadvertent release
  3. Marker 3: Accurate reporting measuring inadvertent releases
  4. Marker 4: Decreasing trend in inadvertent releases observed

Implementation Tracking:

Path between markers = Sequential activities

Each activity = Series of actions assigned to specific people/teams

Format = Gantt-style work plan with dependencies

📊 Component 2: Measuring Capability (DRA)

Digital Resilience Assessment (DRA) Methodology ▶

How DRA Ensures Objectivity:

Structure of Questions

Never asks "how good are you" - Instead: "which of the following things do you do"

Nature of Respondents

  • Multiple people from single organization participate
  • Provides increased granularity across business units
  • Averages away individual biases
  • Variations lead to productive discussions

Validation Process

Review responses with participants, asking why they responded as they did

Result: Integrated, holistic, granular and actionable view of protection capabilities without undue cost and complexity

🛡️ Component 3: Measuring Protection

"Crown Jewels" Protection Measurement ▶

Prerequisites for Protection Measurement:

  1. Organization knows what its most important data is ✓
  2. Organization knows what systems that data sits on ✓
  3. Organization knows how those systems are currently protected ✓
  4. Organization aligned on how each type should be protected ✓
Example Protection Discussion

Policy Agreement: High net worth customer data requires:

  • Encryption at rest
  • Two-factor authentication
  • Access validation every 90 days

Current State:

  • Operations: 70% protected to spec ✓
  • Trading: 55% protected to spec ⚠️
  • Distribution: 25% protected to spec ❌

Root Cause: Distribution's main gap is encryption (80% of problem)

Making Metrics Work: Implementation Excellence Part 5 of 6

🚀 From Theory to Practice

📋 Implementing Progress Metrics

Six-Step Implementation Approach

Creating Effective Dashboards ▶

Dashboard Design Principles:

Visual Hierarchy
  • Most critical metrics at top
  • Use color coding consistently
  • Group related metrics
  • Show trends, not just snapshots
Actionable Insights
  • Include context for each metric
  • Show targets alongside actuals
  • Highlight exceptions clearly
  • Enable drill-down capability
Common Implementation Challenges ▶

Challenge 1: Data Quality

Problem: Inconsistent or incomplete data

Solution: Start with manual collection, automate gradually

Challenge 2: Metric Overload

Problem: Too many metrics dilute focus

Solution: Maximum 15-20 key metrics for executives

Challenge 3: Gaming the System

Problem: Teams optimize for metrics, not outcomes

Solution: Balance multiple metric types, focus on outcomes

Challenge 4: Lack of Action

Problem: Metrics reviewed but not acted upon

Solution: Tie metrics to specific owners and review cadence

🔄 Continuous Improvement Process

Monthly Review Cycle:

  1. Week 1: Collect and validate data
  2. Week 2: Analyze trends and exceptions
  3. Week 3: Review with stakeholders
  4. Week 4: Implement corrective actions
