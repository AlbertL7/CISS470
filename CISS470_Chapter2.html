🎯 The CEO Can't Delegate Cyber Accountability

Tara to Tom: "No, you own cybersecurity; we oversee it alongside the board... I don't mean our IT approach, I mean our whole-of-organization capability to manage cyber threats."

The Fundamental Shift

This type of clarity and direction to CEOs is relatively new, but gaining traction globally. The key question well-intending boards need to ask is: "What specifically do we and the organization's CEO need to do differently to meet these new cybersecurity expectations?"

🤔 Board Reality Check

📋 Three Critical Questions This Chapter Addresses

Question 1: What are boards expected to do now? ▶

The Short Answer: "It depends" on multiple factors:

  • What country your organization is in
  • The focus and approach of regulators in that country
  • The business sector the organization is in
  • The evolution of legal duty of care
  • The frequency of major governance crises linked to cybersecurity breaches
  • The culture of the organization

But the universal answer can be summarized as: "A lot more, but NOT more of the same."

Question 2: What barriers will well-intending boards face? ▶

Five Main Categories of Barriers:

  1. Lack of senior management ownership of IT security
  2. Failure to link cybersecurity assessments to key organization objectives
  3. Omission of cybersecurity from entity-level objectives and strategic plans
  4. Too much focus on internal controls
  5. Lack of reliable information on residual risk status

These barriers prevent boards from getting the clear picture they need for effective oversight.

Question 3: What practical steps should boards take now? ▶

Four Practical Steps for Immediate Action:

  1. Use a "Five Lines of Assurance" approach
  2. Include top objectives and specific owners
  3. Establish a risk management framework
  4. Require regular reporting by the CEO

⚠️ Warning: The steps proposed in this chapter are described as "a radical departure from status quo thinking."

📚 The Ocean of Advice Problem

The chapter acknowledges that boards face "a veritable ocean of advice" on cybersecurity. This creates:

  • Information overload and decision paralysis
  • Conflicting recommendations from different sources
  • Difficulty distinguishing critical actions from nice-to-haves
  • Tendency to default to traditional approaches that don't work

Solution: Focus on fundamental changes to risk governance, not incremental improvements to existing approaches.

What Boards Are Expected to Do: The NACD Framework Part 2 of 5

📋 The NACD's Five Core Principles for Directors

Background: The 2014 Wake-Up Call

The National Association of Corporate Directors (NACD) recognized the emerging need for director guidance following a flurry of major scandals involving IT security breaches. They produced a "Cyber Risk Oversight" guide available at https://www.nacdonline.org/cyber

🎯 The Five Core Principles - Click Each to Explore

1 Cybersecurity as Enterprise Risk (THE KEY PRINCIPLE)

What it means: Directors need to understand and approach cybersecurity as an enterprise risk management (ERM) issue, not just an IT issue.

Why it's #1: The authors specifically note this is "the key principle" - it fundamentally reframes how boards should think about cyber risk.

Implications:

  • Cyber risk affects all business functions
  • Must be integrated with overall risk management
  • Cannot be delegated to IT alone
  • Requires enterprise-wide response
2 Understanding Legal Implications

What it means: Directors should understand the legal implications of cyber risks as they relate to their organization's specific circumstances.

Key considerations:

  • Director and officer liability
  • Regulatory compliance requirements
  • Breach notification laws
  • Litigation risks
  • Contractual obligations

Action required: Boards should seek legal counsel specific to their industry and jurisdiction.

3 Access to Expertise & Regular Agenda Time

What it means: Boards should have adequate access to cybersecurity expertise, and discussions about cyber risk management should be given regular and adequate time on board meeting agendas.

Two critical components:

  1. Expertise Access:
    • Board members with cyber knowledge
    • Regular briefings from CISO/experts
    • External advisors when needed
  2. Meeting Time:
    • Standing agenda item
    • Adequate time allocation
    • Not just crisis response
4 Enterprise-Wide Framework Expectations

What it means: Directors should set the expectation that management will establish an enterprise-wide cyber risk management framework with adequate staffing and budget.

Board's role:

  • Set clear expectations for management
  • Ensure adequate resources are allocated
  • Require comprehensive framework, not piecemeal approach
  • Monitor implementation progress

Framework requirements:

  • Enterprise-wide scope
  • Adequate staffing levels
  • Sufficient budget allocation
  • Clear accountability structure
5 Risk Treatment Decisions & Insurance

What it means: Board-management discussions should include identification of which risks to avoid, accept, mitigate, or transfer through insurance, with specific plans for each approach.

Risk treatment options:

  • Avoid: Eliminate activities that create unacceptable risk
  • Accept: Consciously retain risk within appetite
  • Mitigate: Implement controls to reduce risk
  • Transfer: Use insurance or contracts to shift risk

Board responsibilities:

  • Define risk appetite for the organization
  • Approve likelihood and impact scales
  • May be involved in insurance decisions based on value
  • Ensure management addresses risks above threshold
📚 Additional Resources: NIST Cybersecurity Framework ▶

Framework for Improving Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity v1.0

The chapter references the widely acclaimed NIST framework, noting:

  • Strength: Promotes cybersecurity as subset of ERM
  • Weakness: Doesn't emphasize board's role
  • Gap: Lacks practical advice on transitioning from silo-based to integrated approach

Key Observation: The NIST framework proposes an "integrated risk management program" but doesn't provide enough guidance on HOW to achieve this integration.

The Five Barriers: Why Boards Struggle with Cyber Oversight Part 3 of 5

🚧 Barriers to Effective Board Action

The Implementation Challenge

Most boards will face significant difficulty as they attempt to address cyber risk management. Understanding these barriers is essential to overcoming them.

Barrier 1 Lack of Senior Management Ownership

The Problem:

  • IT security seen as IT department's and internal audit's problem
  • CEO and C-suite don't take ownership
  • Treated as separate silo with work done by IT, internal audit, and consultants
  • Specialists often lack "big picture" perspectives

The Impact:

  • No clear line management ownership for risk reporting
  • ERM programs relegated to periodic updates, not continuous process
  • Management doesn't actively participate in identifying cyber risks
  • Boards don't know which objectives are threatened

Critical Insight:

"Senior management is ultimately responsible for all major threats to an organization, so it is critical that the C-suite takes ownership."

Barrier 2 Failure to Link to Key Organization Objectives

The Problem:

  • Cybersecurity work not directly tied to specific organization objectives
  • Boards not told which value creation objectives are impacted
  • IT security promoted as objective in its own right
  • Regulators sometimes push security without cost-benefit analysis

Board Perspective:

Boards are populated with pragmatic executives who focus on key business objectives. Without clear linkage to these objectives, they struggle to:

  • Decide resource allocation
  • Assess risk tolerance
  • Prioritize investments
  • Measure effectiveness

Current State:

Based on IIA surveys globally, only a small percentage of internal audit, IT security, and ERM specialists link their work directly to the organization's top value creation objectives.

Barrier 3 Omission from Entity-Level Objectives

The Disconnect:

  • Cybersecurity included in risk registers but not strategic plans
  • Not integrated into highest-level organizational objectives
  • IT security functions focus exclusively on technical security
  • Risk/audit universes carved out separately from business objectives

Why This Matters:

"In order for cybersecurity to be robust and overarching, it must be included in objectives and strategic plans at the highest level of the organization."

The Result:

Cyber risk remains disconnected from business strategy, making it difficult to get executive attention and resources.

Barrier 4 Too Much Focus on Internal Controls

The Control Fixation:

  • ERM and internal audit focus primarily on identifying controls
  • Binary approach (deficient/not deficient) like Sarbanes-Oxley 404
  • Not aligned with ISO 31000:2009 Risk Management principles
  • Limited to control assessment rather than full risk treatment

Full Range of Risk Treatment Options (ISO 31000):

  1. Avoiding the activity that gives rise to risk
  2. Taking or increasing risk to pursue opportunity
  3. Removing the risk source
  4. Changing the likelihood
  5. Changing the consequences
  6. Sharing risk with other parties
  7. Retaining risk by informed decision

Critical Gap:

Most importantly, organizations fail to evaluate whether accepting residual risk is appropriate given the board's risk appetite and tolerance.

Barrier 5 Lack of Reliable Information on Residual Risk

Information Boards Need But Don't Get:

  • Which critical objectives are impacted by cyber risks?
  • How well are objectives being achieved with current risk treatment?
  • What are potential impacts (reputation, cost, etc.) if objectives fail?
  • What viable risk treatments exist that aren't being used?
  • What do current performance and risk indicators show?
  • What impediments does management face?

The Problem:

Without this information, boards cannot:

  • Assess if current security levels are appropriate
  • Determine if investments are cost-justified
  • Make informed risk acceptance decisions
  • Provide meaningful oversight

🔍 The Compound Effect

These barriers don't exist in isolation—they reinforce each other:

  • Without ownership (Barrier 1): No one drives objective linkage (Barrier 2)
  • Without objective linkage (Barrier 2): Can't get into strategic plans (Barrier 3)
  • With only control focus (Barrier 4): Miss broader risk treatment options
  • Without good information (Barrier 5): Can't demonstrate need for change

Result: A self-reinforcing cycle of ineffective cyber risk management

Four Practical Steps: The Radical Departure Part 4 of 5

✅ Practical Steps Boards Should Take Now

⚠️ Radical Change Required

These four steps represent a fundamental departure from traditional approaches. They require boards to actively drive change, not passively receive reports.

Step 1

Use a "Five Lines of Assurance" Approach

The Five Lines Model

Line 1: Work Units

Frontline operations and risk owners

Line 2: C-Suite

Senior management oversight

Line 3: Specialist Units

Risk, compliance, security functions

Line 4: Internal Audit

Independent assurance

Line 5: Board of Directors

Ultimate oversight and governance

Key Changes from Traditional Model:

  • Board as active participant: Not just recipient of reports
  • CEO accountability: Responsible for consolidated reporting on residual risk
  • All lines provide information: Direct channels to board
  • Combined assurance: Coordinated across all lines

What Must Stop:

  • Senior management not completing formal risk assessments
  • ERM groups building plans around disconnected "risk registers"
  • Internal audit using "audit universes" as primary foundation
  • Point-in-time audits with subjective "control effectiveness" opinions
Step 2

Include Top Objectives and Specific Owners

Objectives Register Requirements:

  • Include top value creation AND preservation objectives
  • Specify owners and sponsors at highest organizational levels
  • Clearly define risk assessment rigor required
  • Establish combined assurance levels

C-Suite Leadership Role:

"The C-suite should take the lead deciding which objectives warrant the cost of formal risk treatment, combined assurance work, and inclusion in the organization's objectives register."

What Goes in the Register:

Value Creation Objectives
  • Highest potential to increase entity value
  • Strategic growth initiatives
  • Innovation programs
Value Preservation Objectives
  • Highest potential to erode value if failed
  • Critical operations
  • Reputation protection

Critical Requirement:

Each objective must have an owner/sponsor with primary responsibility for assessing and reporting upward on residual risk status on a real-time basis.

Step 3

Establish a Risk Management Framework

Framework Purpose:

Provide reliable information so decision makers fully understand the composite residual risk status linked to top objectives.

Framework Requirements:

  • Align with ISO 31000:2009 Risk Management principles
  • Direct linkage of risks to organization objectives
  • Focus on residual risk status, not just controls
  • Composite Residual Risk Rating (CRRR) for each objective

The Risk Status Approach (Figure 2.2)

Key Elements:

  1. Objective owners complete risk assessments
  2. Apply specified levels of rigor (defined by C-suite/board)
  3. Report Composite Residual Risk Rating (CRRR)
  4. Communicate when high residual risk is being accepted
  5. Identify cases where no additional treatments are warranted despite high risk

Critical Communication:

When owners believe no additional risk treatments are warranted but significant residual risk remains, this MUST be communicated to the board, including cases of high cybersecurity residual risk.

Step 4

Require Regular Reporting by the CEO

CEO Accountability Mechanism:

"If, ultimately, the CEO is to be held accountable for cybersecurity, he or she must be fully aware of how the program is working."

Reporting Requirements:

  • Frequency: Regular basis, ideally quarterly
  • Format: Consolidated report on residual risk status
  • Scope: All formal organizational objectives
  • Validation: Internal audit reports on reliability

What the Report Must Include:

  • ✓ Objectives with residual risk outside appetite/tolerance
  • ✓ Actions being taken to address excessive risks
  • ✓ Areas where high residual risk is being accepted
  • ✓ Cyber risks in context of business objectives impacted
  • ✓ Impediments management faces
  • ✓ Performance and risk indicators

Internal Audit's Role:

Internal audit should independently assess and report on the reliability of the CEO's consolidated report, providing assurance to the board.

🔄 The Integration Challenge

These four steps work together as an integrated system:

  • Five Lines (Step 1) provides the governance structure
  • Objectives Register (Step 2) defines what to protect
  • Framework (Step 3) provides the methodology
  • CEO Reporting (Step 4) ensures accountability

Critical Success Factor: All four must be implemented together for the system to work effectively.

The Way Forward: Call to Action Part 5 of 5

🚀 Cybersecurity—The Way Forward

The Imperative for Radical Change

"The way forward, if real progress is to be achieved, requires major changes in the way that a large percentage of organizations have historically approached risk governance generally, and cybersecurity in particular."

🔄 Why Radical Change Is Necessary ▶

The Current State Isn't Working:

  • Cyber breaches continue to increase despite investment
  • Boards remain unprepared for cyber oversight
  • Siloed approaches create gaps and inefficiencies
  • Traditional audit and control focus misses the point

What "Radical Change" Means:

  • Not incremental improvements to existing processes
  • Not more of the same specialist-driven approaches
  • Not treating cyber as another silo to manage
  • Complete reengineering of risk governance

Key Quote: "Cyber risks should not continue to be treated as yet another silo."

📋 Who Must Embrace Change

External Stakeholders

  • Regulators: Move beyond compliance checklists
  • Professional Associations: Update standards and guidance
  • Consultants: Shift from silo to enterprise approach
  • Auditors: Focus on objectives, not just controls

Internal Stakeholders

  • Boards of Directors: Drive governance change
  • Senior Management: Accept ownership
  • Internal Auditors: Evolve beyond traditional audit
  • Risk Specialists: Integrate with business
📊 The Change Management Strategy ▶

Required Elements:

  1. Recognition: Accept that current approaches aren't working
  2. Vision: Clear picture of integrated risk management
  3. Leadership: Champions at board and executive level
  4. Resources: Investment in transformation
  5. Persistence: Sustained effort over time

Starting Small:

"Like many big undertakings, change needs to start with some small steps."

First Steps:

  • Advocate for change in your organization
  • Challenge traditional approaches
  • Pilot new methods with critical objectives
  • Demonstrate value through early wins
  • Build momentum for broader change
