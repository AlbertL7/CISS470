Policy 1: Social Media Risk Management Part 2 of 6

📱 Social Media: The Overlooked Risk

Case Study: McDonald's #McDStories Disaster

McDonald's created hashtag #McDStories to encourage positive stories. Instead, users hijacked it to share negative experiences, derailing the campaign and embarrassing the company.

Lesson: Social media campaigns can backfire spectacularly without proper risk assessment and response planning.

🎯 Two Key Risk Areas

1. Employee Use Risks

  • Intellectual property leakage
  • Data exposure
  • Virus introduction
  • Password compromise
  • Reputation damage from personal posts

2. Corporate Account Risks

  • Negative posts/comments
  • Campaign backfiring
  • Inefficient use of resources
  • Brand impersonation
  • Crisis amplification
📝 Six Steps to Prepare Your Social Media Policy ▶
  1. Engage a multidisciplinary team: HR, Legal, IT, Risk, Marketing
  2. Clarify objectives: Reputation, talent, sales, or engagement?
  3. Undertake risk assessment: Identify specific threats and impacts
  4. Obtain senior mandate: CEO and board buy-in essential
  5. Understand legal implications: Monitoring, discipline, privacy laws
  6. Train all staff: Policy useless without proper training

📋 Two Essential Policies Recommended

Policy 1: Personal Social Media Use by Employees ▶

Key Sections to Include:

Introduction: Organization objectives for social media program
Definitions: What constitutes IP, confidential info, competitors
Boundaries: Approval processes, opinion ranges, political restrictions
Off-Limits Content: Confidential info, competitor comments, illegal content
Safe Use: Password updates, privacy settings, anti-virus
Personal Use: Can employees use company Internet? Disclaimer requirements
Reporting: Email address for reporting concerning content
Monitoring: Transparency about employee monitoring practices
Consequences: Disciplinary procedures for violations
Policy 2: Corporate Social Media Accounts ▶

Key Sections to Include:

Responsibilities: Who manages accounts, posts, and monitors brand use
Customer Comments: Do's and don'ts for responding, pre-approved messages
Fake Accounts: Steps for takedown requests, legal contacts
Promotions/Competitions: Terms compliance, cheating prevention
💭 Scenario: The Disgruntled Employee

An employee posts on their personal Twitter: "My company's new product is garbage. Can't believe we're charging customers for this." What should you do?

📱 Social Media Key Takeaway: Organizations need TWO policies minimum—one for personal use, one for corporate accounts. Focus on behavior guidance rather than restrictive rules, and ensure all employees understand their responsibility for protecting the organization's reputation.
Policy 2: Ransomware Risk Policies and Procedures Part 3 of 6

🔒 Ransomware: The Year 2016's Biggest Threat

2016: "The Year of Ransomware"

$200M+

Reported victim costs in just Q1 2016

🚨 High-Profile Ransomware Attacks

Recent Attack Examples
  • U.S. Police Departments: Lost data on open cases
  • University of Calgary: Forced to pay C$20,000 after all emails/files encrypted
  • Hollywood Hospital, LA: Paid $17,000 after facing $3.4M extortion demand
🦠 How Cybercriminals Spread Ransomware ▶
  • Spam email campaigns: Mass distribution to thousands
  • Software vulnerabilities: Exploiting unpatched systems
  • Internet traffic redirection: From legitimate to malicious sites
  • SMS messages: Targeting mobile devices
  • Legitimate website injection: Malicious code in trusted sites
  • Drive-by downloads: Automatic infection from compromised sites

Key Policy Philosophy

Frame policies on the presumption that it's "WHEN, not IF" your organization will be targeted. Ransomware attacks are sophisticated enough to bypass anti-virus software.

🛡️ Ransomware Policy Key Content

1. Back Up Data Regularly

Daily, weekly, or monthly backups to limit impact and speed recovery

2. Segregate Your Data

Store in different locations to prevent total loss from single attack

3. Keep Antivirus Updated

Automate updates where possible, implement immediately when manual

4. Best-in-Class Anti-Spam

First layer of defense against phishing emails

5. Strong Passwords

Minimum length, upper/lowercase, no names/birthdays, regular changes

6. Blacklisting Software

Block categories: adult content, criminal activity, gambling, hate, violence, phishing

7. Limit Application Use

Standardized, restrictive set of essential work applications only

8. Principle of "Least Privilege"

Restrict access to only critical folders/data required for job role

🎓 Education, Education, Education ▶
  • Run phishing tests every 3-6 months minimum
  • Mandatory training for all new employees
  • Training modules with sample suspicious emails
  • Employee-created awareness posters
  • Clear escalation procedures for suspicious emails
  • Instructions on marking email headers to prevent spread

Policies 3 & 4: Cloud Computing and Big Data Analytics Part 4 of 6

☁️ Cloud Computing & Third-Party Vendor Policies

✅ Cloud Benefits vs. ⚠️ Risks

Key Benefits

  • Flexibility: Remote access, scalable resources
  • Cost savings: Pay-per-use models, no infrastructure
  • Reliability: 24/7 support, economies of scale
  • Enhanced security: Often exceeds internal capabilities

Primary Risks

  • Internet dependency: Outages disrupt business
  • Data concentration: All eggs in one basket
  • Contract risks: Weak agreements, hidden liabilities
  • Loss of control: Third-party safeguarding
📋 Procuring Cloud Services Effectively ▶
  1. Identify desired outcomes: Issue clear ITT with objectives
  2. Review standard contracts: Rank by favorability with legal help
  3. Be selective: Only providers meeting your conditions
  4. Scope precontract: Agree transition plan and scenarios
  5. Draft carefully: Incentivized payments, remove "agreements to agree"
  6. Clarify terms: Understand liability, choice of law, data location

🔑 Customer Agreement Key Content to Negotiate

Limitations of Liability: Ensure aggregate limit isn't capped too low, protect against gross negligence
Disaster Recovery: Provider aware of your RTO, data in multiple locations
Terms & Termination: 30+ days notice, data retention during transition
Service Suspension: Minimum 60 days notice
Service Level Agreement: Availability guarantees, automated outage logging
Security & Privacy: Data location control, encryption, breach notification

📊 Big Data Analytics Policy

EU GDPR Coming May 2018

4%

Maximum fine of global turnover for violations

🎯 Two Main Big Data Threats

1. Privacy Breach Risk

Aggregation of huge personal data volumes creates massive exposure if breached

2. Regulatory Compliance

GDPR requires "privacy by design" approach with severe penalties for non-compliance

🔐 Big Data "Privacy by Design" Key Content ▶
Delete Redundant Data: Remove data no longer relevant for analytics
Best-in-Class Antivirus: Regular/automated updates and patches
Encrypt Data: Both at rest and in transit (end-to-end encryption)
Spread Storage: Multiple locations minimize breach impact
Lawful Collection: Ensure explicit consent and cross-border compliance
PCI Compliance: For payment data - avoid costly assessments
Third-Party Vendors: Due diligence on security, tight contracts
👥 Employee Policy Amendments for Big Data ▶
  • Education: Regular tests on data protection laws with scenarios
  • USB/External Storage: Prevent personal USBs, encrypt authorized ones
  • Password Management: Never store keys in accessible places
  • BYOD Compliance: Two-factor authentication on personal devices
☁️ Contract Negotiation

A major cloud provider offers great pricing but their standard contract caps liability at $10,000 and requires arbitration in another country. What should you prioritize?

☁️ Cloud & Big Data Key Takeaway: Success depends on robust procurement processes and tight contracts for cloud services, plus "privacy by design" principles for big data. Both require understanding that you remain liable even when using third parties.

🌐 Internet of Things (IoT) Policy

McKinsey Global Estimates

$6.2T

Potential IoT value by 2025

50 billion connected devices by 2020

🚨 IoT Attack Examples

Real-World IoT Breaches
  • 2015 Fiat Chrysler: Recalled 1.4 million vehicles after wireless hack demonstration
  • 2014 German Steel Mill: Blast furnace suffered massive damage after hackers accessed controls through employee emails
⚠️ Categories of IoT Threats ▶
  • Data Protection: Massive personal data gathering creates identifiability risks
  • Device Proliferation: More connected devices = expanded attack surface
  • Speed of Change: Security controls lag behind connectivity growth
  • Outage Risk: Traffic volumes can overwhelm servers
  • Security Lags: Unencrypted links between devices common

IoT Policy Key Content

Identify all stakeholders: Regulators, individuals, device users, public, data owners
Worst-case scenarios: Document and plan for catastrophic failures
End-to-end encryption: From data center to endpoint
Network segregation: Separate IoT from critical corporate data
Device mapping: Identify all connected devices and data flows
Usage documentation: Clear guidelines for permissible device uses

📱 BYOD/Mobile Device Policy

The BYOD Revolution

85% of companies now allow BYOD, driven by:

  • Employee demand for flexibility
  • Lower hardware costs for companies
  • Increased productivity claims
  • Work-life integration trends

⚡ Key BYOD Risks

  • Data breach through personal devices (accidental or intentional)
  • Unsecured network connections
  • Theft of corporate IP and sensitive data
  • Mixing of personal and corporate data
  • Data interception between device and systems
  • Device loss or theft
  • Malware infection spreading to network
  • Privacy regulation violations when used abroad
📋 BYOD Policy Options Spectrum ▶
  1. Disallow BYOD: Ultimate risk avoidance but may frustrate employees
  2. "Do Nothing" Approach: Dangerous - leads to data leakage and loss of control
  3. Corporate Devices Only: More control but higher costs
  4. Managed BYOD Policy (Recommended): Balance flexibility with security controls
🔑 BYOD Policy Key Content ▶

Acceptable Use

  • Compliance with all company policies
  • Website blocking during work hours
  • Restrictions on storing illicit material
  • Zero tolerance for texting while driving

Devices and Support

  • Choose-your-own-device (CYOD) from approved list
  • IT verification before BYOD permission
  • Supported models (iPhone, Android versions)

Security Requirements

  • Minimum password lengths with capitals/symbols
  • Auto-lock after idle period
  • Two-factor authentication
  • Encryption for stored data
  • Remote wipe capability

Risks/Liabilities

  • Immediate reporting if lost/stolen
  • Employee liability for data loss
  • Right to disconnect/disable device
  • Backup responsibilities

🔗 Common Themes Across All Six Policies ▶
  • Employee Responsibility: All policies emphasize this is NOT just IT's problem
  • Education Critical: Training more important than technology
  • Multidisciplinary Approach: HR, Legal, IT, Risk must collaborate
  • Risk Assessment First: Understand threats before writing policies
  • Senior Management Buy-in: CEO ownership essential
  • Balance Required: Security vs. productivity/innovation
  • Continuous Evolution: Policies must adapt to changing threats
  • Clear Consequences: Disciplinary procedures must be defined
📊 Maturity Considerations ▶

The chapter emphasizes continuous improvement through the risk maturity curve:

  1. Initial: Ad-hoc policies if any exist
  2. Developing: Basic policies drafted but inconsistently applied
  3. Defined: Comprehensive policies with training programs
  4. Managed: Regular testing, metrics, and updates
  5. Optimizing: Continuous improvement based on threat intelligence

"A cycle of continuous improvement throughout the organization allows development along the risk maturity curve. The policies provide a platform for companies to maximize digital opportunities while managing the threats."

🎓 Final Scenario: Policy Prioritization

You're the new CISO at a mid-size company with NO formal cyber policies. You can only implement one policy this quarter due to resource constraints. Which do you choose?

⚠️ Critical Implementation Advice

  • Don't try to implement all six policies at once
  • Start with highest risk areas for your organization
  • Get employee buy-in through involvement, not mandate
  • Test policies through tabletop exercises
  • Review and update at least annually
  • Make policies accessible and understandable
  • Focus on behavior, not just compliance
